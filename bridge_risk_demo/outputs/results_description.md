# Results Description

## 1. Conference Abstract Bullets

- Approximately 61.9% of Texas's 56,951 NBI-listed structures scored in the High or Critical risk tiers under a composite metric weighting structural condition (0.30), age relative to a 50-year design life (0.30), and traffic exposure (0.40), indicating that nearly two-thirds of the inventory warrants prioritised inspection or intervention planning.

- Only 1.2% of structures currently carry FHWA Poor condition ratings (minimum component rating of 4 or below), yet 43.8% have exceeded their 50-year notional design life, suggesting that the state faces a latent risk wave as aging bridges with acceptable present-day ratings continue to deteriorate.

- 18 bridges classified as Poor carry more than 50,000 vehicles per day, including segments of IH-45 and IH-35E in the Houston and Dallas--Fort Worth metropolitan areas built between 1954 and 1971, concentrating the highest consequence-of-failure risk on a small number of interstate corridors.

## 2. Recommended Figure for Journal Submission

Figure 2 (Bridge Age vs. Structural Condition) is the strongest candidate for a journal submission. It encodes three variables simultaneously -- age, minimum condition rating, and composite risk score -- allowing the reader to see the deterioration trajectory of the entire inventory in a single panel. The FHWA Poor threshold and 50-year design-life reference lines partition the scatter into four interpretable quadrants, making the key policy finding immediately legible: the upper-right quadrant (old but still in satisfactory condition) is densely populated, visually demonstrating the latent risk that abstract statistics alone do not convey. Unlike the risk map, which requires geographic familiarity, Figure 2 communicates to any infrastructure audience without additional context.

## 3. Most Surprising Finding

**Finding:** Despite 43.8% of bridges exceeding a 50-year design life, only 1.5% of those aged structures are rated Poor. The median risk score (0.55) sits slightly above the mean (0.52), with the bulk of the inventory concentrated in the High tier. With traffic weighted at 40%, the highest-risk bridges are now dominated by high-ADT urban interstate segments rather than rural structures.

**Follow-up question:** Are the condition ratings of these older bridges genuinely stable, or has sustained maintenance and rehabilitation activity masked underlying deterioration that would become apparent under reduced funding scenarios? A longitudinal analysis comparing year-on-year rating trajectories for the pre-1975 cohort against maintenance expenditure records would clarify whether the current condition profile reflects durable structural health or deferred risk.

## 4. Geographic Risk Pattern Interpretation

The risk map reveals a clear east--west gradient across Texas, with the highest-risk structures concentrated in the eastern third of the state, roughly east of the I-35 corridor. This region -- spanning the Piney Woods, Gulf Coast, and Post Oak Belt -- contains the state's oldest road networks, many built during the 1930s--1960s federal highway expansion era, and its bridge inventory reflects that construction vintage. The pattern intensifies along rural farm-to-market road systems in counties such as Polk, Houston, and Limestone, where low traffic volumes have historically attracted less capital reinvestment than urban interstates. By contrast, the western half of the state shows predominantly green and yellow (low to moderate risk), consistent with its sparser, newer road network built to serve post-war oil and gas development and later interstate construction. Within the metropolitan areas of Dallas--Fort Worth, Houston, San Antonio, and Austin, the pattern is mixed: recent construction on urban freeways lowers the average, but legacy interstate segments from the 1950s--1960s appear as isolated high-risk clusters embedded within otherwise lower-risk urban networks. The geographic concentration suggests that a regionally targeted inspection and rehabilitation programme focused on rural East Texas could address a disproportionate share of the state's structural risk inventory.
